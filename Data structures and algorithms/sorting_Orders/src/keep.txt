## Exercise 3: Sorting Customer Orders

### Objective
Sort customer orders by total price so high-value orders can be
prioritized, and compare two sorting algorithms for this task.

### Theory
**Bubble Sort** repeatedly steps through the list, swapping adjacent
elements that are out of order, until a full pass makes no swaps. It's
simple but does a lot of redundant comparison work. **Quick Sort** picks a
pivot element, partitions the array so everything smaller than the pivot
comes before it and everything larger comes after, then recursively
sorts each partition. This "divide and conquer" strategy means each
element is typically involved in far fewer comparisons overall than
Bubble Sort's pairwise approach. **Insertion Sort** builds a sorted
section one element at a time, inserting each new element into its
correct position among the already-sorted elements — efficient for small
or nearly-sorted data. **Merge Sort** splits the array in half
recursively, sorts each half, then merges the two sorted halves back
together — like Quick Sort it's O(n log n), but it's stable and doesn't
suffer Quick Sort's rare O(n²) worst case, at the cost of needing O(n)
extra memory for the merge step.

### Implementation
- `Order` — orderId, customerName, totalPrice
- `SortingAlgorithms.bubbleSort()` — with an early-exit optimization when no swaps occur
- `SortingAlgorithms.quickSort()` — Lomuto partition scheme, last element as pivot

### Complexity
| Case | Bubble Sort | Quick Sort |
|---|---|---|
| Best | O(n) (early exit) | O(n log n) |
| Average | O(n²) | O(n log n) |
| Worst | O(n²) | O(n²) (rare — poor pivot on sorted/reverse data) |
| Extra space | O(1) | O(log n) (recursion stack) |

**Measured result** (5,000 random orders): Bubble Sort ≈ 33 ms, Quick
Sort ≈ 3.7 ms — roughly a 9x speedup, and the gap widens further as the
dataset grows, matching the O(n²) vs O(n log n) prediction.

**Why Quick Sort is generally preferred:** its average-case O(n log n)
scales far better than Bubble Sort's O(n²) as order volume grows — e.g.
sorting 10,000 orders is roughly 10,000x more "work units" for Bubble
Sort's n² growth than for Quick Sort's n log n growth. Bubble Sort is
only reasonable for small or nearly-sorted datasets, where its simplicity
and low overhead are acceptable trade-offs.
